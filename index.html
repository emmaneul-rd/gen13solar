Solar panels convert sunlight into direct-current electricity. An inverter converts that power into alternating current your home can use. Depending on your design, excess production may flow to the utility grid or charge a battery.

Many modern panels are designed to operate for 25 years or more, although output gradually declines and warranties vary by manufacturer. Inverters and other components may have different replacement cycles.

No. Grid-tied solar can operate without storage. Batteries can add backup capability, time-of-use flexibility and more control, but the right choice depends on budget, outage concerns and utility rules.

Yes, though production is usually lower than under direct sun. Annual energy modeling accounts for local weather patterns rather than assuming every day is perfectly clear.

Owned solar may contribute to buyer interest and perceived operating savings, but market impact varies. Financing structure, system condition, documentation and local appraisal practices all matter.

The right size depends on annual usage, roof area, orientation, shade, panel output, future loads such as EV charging, utility limits and your financial goals. Bigger is not automatically better.
